Abstract

This project presents a study on pose-based SLAM using EKF for robot localization, specifically focusing on a differential drive robot equipped with Lidar, IMU, and wheel velocity sensors. The mapping algorithm utilized is the Iterative Closest Point (ICP), while the Extended Kalman filter is employed for state estimation. The report outlines the robot motion model and observations model used in the SLAM algorithm. The implementation details, experimental evaluation, and results are discussed, highlighting the effectiveness of pose-based SLAM in achieving accurate robot localization and mapping in real-world environments.

Methodology



The system consists of wheel sensors that capture wheel velocities, which are then utilized as inputs for the prediction state of the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF). The EKF generates predicted states, which are subsequently fed into theobservation model. The observation model is responsible for generating expected measurements based on the predictedstates. Simultaneously, the Lidar sensors capture scans, which are processed by the Iterative Closest Point (ICP) algorithm. The ICP algorithm produces measurements based on the scans. These measurements, along with the expected measurements from the observation model, are then passed to the update state of the EKF. By integrating the expected and measured data, the update state of the EKF constructs a map. This map contains information about poses (positions and orientations) as well as covariance estimates. Overall, this system architecture enables the derivation of accurate poses and covariance information from the generated map.

Initially, the map generated by dead reckoning exhibited a close alignment with the ground truth map. However, as time progressed, the map started to drift gradually, resulting in increasing misalignment. This behavior can be attributed to the accumulation of uncertainty and error in the wheel sensors. Over time, the uncertainty in the estimated robot pose by dead reckoning increased, leading to a divergence from the actual ground truth position of the robot. In contrast, SLAM consistently provides stable and ac- curate estimates of the ground truth states. The map generated by SLAM remains in close proximity to the ground truth map. This improved performance can be attributed to the additional correction step in the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF)-based SLAM. While dead reckoning solely relies on prediction, SLAM incorporates a correction step that effectively reduces the accumulated error and uncertainty during the prediction phase. During the correction step, all registered poses of the robot are simultaneously updated, leveraging the intercorrelations between the poses. This si- multaneous update of poses in SLAM significantly enhances the accuracy and robustness of the estimated trajectory.
